FCA Cryptoasset Rules 2026 — What UK Investors Need to Know

The new regulatory regime explained: timeline, protections, and what actually changes

Key Takeaways
  • The UK cryptoasset regulatory regime comes into force on 25 October 2027 — it's law, not speculation.
  • Firms must be FCA-authorised to operate trading platforms, provide custody, or offer staking services.
  • Retail investors get new protections: 10% cap, appropriateness tests, 24-hour cooling-off, enhanced warnings.
  • Public offers require a Cryptoasset Admission and Disclosure Document (like a prospectus).
  • Market abuse rules now apply to cryptoassets — insider dealing and manipulation are prohibited.
  • Private placements to certified high-net-worth or sophisticated investors remain exempt from full disclosure.

What's Happening

The UK is implementing a comprehensive regulatory regime for cryptoassets. This isn't speculation—it's law. The Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (Cryptoassets) Regulations 2026 were made by Parliament on 4 February 2026. The new regime comes into force on 25 October 2027.

Between now and then, firms must prepare for authorisation. Investors need to understand what changes, what stays the same, and how they're protected.

The Four Consultation Papers

The FCA published four major consultation papers in December 2025. Together, they form the backbone of the new regime:

Core Regulations
CP25/28: Quarterly consultation (broader financial services context)
CP25/40: Regulating cryptoasset activities (trading platforms, intermediaries, staking, DeFi)
CP25/41: Admissions, disclosures, and market abuse regime for cryptoassets
CP25/42: Prudential regime for cryptoasset firms (capital requirements, operational resilience)

All four consultations closed in February 2026. The FCA will publish final policy statements later in 2026, ahead of the October 2027 implementation.

What Activities Require Authorisation

Under the new regime, firms conducting the following activities in or to the UK must be FCA-authorised:

  • Operating a cryptoasset trading platform (exchanges, matching buyers and sellers)
  • Cryptoasset intermediation (arranging deals, making arrangements with a view to transactions)
  • Cryptoasset lending and borrowing (including staking-as-a-service)
  • Cryptoasset custody (safeguarding cryptoassets on behalf of customers)
  • Stablecoin issuance (regulated separately under earlier consultations)
  • Cryptoasset admissions (admitting cryptoassets to trading platforms)

If a firm does any of these things without authorisation after October 2027, it's breaking the law. The FCA can enforce, fine, and prosecute.

Public Offers vs. Private Placements

One of the biggest changes: how cryptoassets can be offered to investors.

Public Offers

A public offer means marketing cryptoassets to the general public. Under CP25/41, public offers require:

  • Cryptoasset Admission and Disclosure Document (QCDD): Similar to a prospectus for listed securities. Must include risk warnings, project details, token economics, team information, and financial disclosures.
  • Cryptoasset Admission and Trading Platform (CATP) approval: Only authorised platforms can admit cryptoassets for trading.
  • Ongoing disclosure obligations: Material changes must be disclosed to investors.

Private Placements

Private placements (offers to institutional or certified investors) face lighter requirements. Key exemptions:

  • Offers to certified high-net-worth individuals
  • Offers to self-certified sophisticated investors
  • Offers to institutional investors (pension funds, authorised firms, etc.)
  • Offers to fewer than 150 people (excluding qualified investors)

Private placements don't require a full QCDD, but must still comply with financial promotion rules and anti-money laundering requirements.

Retail Investor Protections

Risk is Real
Cryptoassets are high-risk. Most retail investors lose money. The FCA's rules acknowledge this reality and aim to protect consumers from irreversible harm.

1. The 10% Cap

Ordinary retail consumers should not invest more than 10% of their net assets in high-risk investments, including cryptoassets. This rule already exists in the financial promotions regime and continues under the new framework.

Firms must assess whether a customer understands this limit, provide clear warnings about concentration risk, and not encourage investments exceeding this threshold.

2. Appropriateness Assessments

Before a retail investor can trade cryptoassets, firms must assess whether the investment is appropriate for them. This includes knowledge and understanding, experience with high-risk investments, and financial situation.

If an investor fails the appropriateness test, the firm must warn them. Some firms may refuse service entirely.

3. 24-Hour Cooling-Off Period

New retail customers must wait 24 hours between signing up and making their first cryptoasset trade. This cooling-off period cannot be waived or expedited, gives investors time to read risk warnings, and reduces impulse trading.

4. Enhanced Risk Warnings

Generic "capital at risk" warnings are no longer sufficient. The FCA wants personalised risk warnings that explain specific risks of the cryptoasset being purchased, highlight the investor's concentration in high-risk assets, and include clear, prominent language.

Market Abuse Regime

CP25/41 extends market abuse rules to cryptoassets. This means insider dealing is prohibited (trading on non-public, price-sensitive information), unlawful disclosure is prohibited (leaking inside information), and market manipulation is prohibited (wash trading, pump-and-dump schemes, spoofing).

The FCA can investigate and prosecute market abuse in cryptoassets just like traditional securities. Penalties include unlimited fines and imprisonment.

Prudential Requirements

Cryptoasset firms must meet capital and operational resilience standards including initial capital (€50,000 to €750,000+ depending on services), ongoing capital based on fixed overheads, safeguarding requirements for customer assets, operational resilience systems, and professional indemnity insurance.

These requirements aim to prevent another FTX-style collapse. Firms must prove they can survive stress scenarios and protect customer funds.

What This Means for Investors

The Good

  • More protection: Appropriateness tests, cooling-off periods, and enhanced warnings reduce impulse decisions
  • Cleaner market: Unauthorised firms exit, reducing fraud risk
  • Clearer recourse: Authorised firms must have complaints procedures; some may join the Financial Ombudsman Service
  • Better disclosures: QCDDs provide standardised information for informed decisions

The Trade-Offs

  • Fewer choices: Some platforms may exit the UK market rather than seek authorisation
  • Higher costs: Compliance costs may be passed to customers through higher fees
  • Slower innovation: New products require regulatory approval, delaying launches
  • DeFi uncertainty: Truly decentralised protocols may become inaccessible if intermediaries can't comply

What Investors Should Do Now

  1. Check if your platform is preparing for authorisation. Most major exchanges (Coinbase, Kraken, etc.) are already compliant with financial promotions rules and will seek full authorisation.
  2. Complete any required certifications. If you're asked to confirm you're a high-net-worth or sophisticated investor, understand what this means legally.
  3. Read the risk warnings. Seriously. The 10% cap exists because most people lose money on crypto. Don't invest rent money.
  4. Keep records. Transaction history, tax calculations, and correspondence may be needed for compliance or tax purposes.
  5. Watch for platform changes. Between now and October 2027, platforms may update terms, restrict services, or change fee structures to comply with new rules.

Bottom Line

The UK's cryptoasset regime is coming. It's not a question of if—it's a question of when. The timeline is set: applications open September 2026, full regime goes live October 2027.

For investors, this means more protection but potentially fewer choices. For firms, it means compliance costs but also legitimacy. For the market overall, it means maturation—cryptoassets treated more like traditional financial instruments, with similar rules and oversight.

Whether this is good or bad depends on your perspective. If you value consumer protection and market integrity, it's progress. If you value permissionless innovation and decentralisation, it's a step back.

Either way, it's happening. Plan accordingly.
