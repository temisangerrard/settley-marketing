Quick Summary

You've heard the term. Maybe you've seen it on Twitter, or in some fintech newsletter. Property tokenisation.

Sounds complicated. Sounds like crypto hype. Sounds like something that probably won't work.

Here's what it actually is: a way to own a slice of a property without needing £30k for a deposit, without dealing with mortgages, without becoming a landlord.

This guide walks through how it works, what the FCA allows, and what could go wrong. No promises. No investment pitch. Just the facts.

What Is Property Tokenisation?

Property tokenisation uses blockchain technology to represent ownership of real estate as digital tokens.

That's the technical definition. Here's what it means in practice:

Traditional Property Ownership

You want to buy a £200k flat in Manchester. You need:

  • £30-40k deposit (15-20%)
  • Mortgage approval (credit checks, income verification, months of paperwork)
  • Solicitor fees (£1,500-2,500)
  • Stamp Duty (depends on price, first-time buyer relief may apply)
  • Full commitment — you're buying the whole thing or nothing

Once you own it, you're the landlord. You deal with tenants, repairs, void periods, the whole thing. When you want to sell, it takes months. Maybe longer if the market's cold.

Tokenised Property Ownership

A £200k flat in Manchester is purchased by a UK Limited Company (called an SPV — Special Purpose Vehicle). The company owns the property. Nothing unusual there.

The company then issues 200,000 tokens. Each token represents 0.0005% ownership of the company, which owns the flat.

You buy 1,000 tokens for £1,000. You now own 0.5% of the company, which owns 0.5% of the property.

When rent comes in, you get 0.5% of it (after expenses). When the property sells, you get 0.5% of the proceeds (after costs).

No mortgage. No tenants calling you at 2am about a burst pipe. No need to save £30k before you can start.

How Does It Work?

Step 1: Property Acquisition

A UK property is purchased by a Special Purpose Vehicle — a standard UK Limited Company registered at Companies House.

Property → Owned by → UK SPV (Limited Company)

The SPV holds legal title. This is normal UK property law. Nothing new, nothing experimental.

Step 2: Token Issuance

The SPV issues digital tokens on a blockchain (Ethereum, Polygon, or similar). Each token represents shares in the company.

UK SPV → Issues → Tokens (on blockchain)

These tokens are recorded on the blockchain — immutable, auditable, traceable. You can see exactly how many tokens exist and who holds them (subject to privacy settings).

Step 3: Token Distribution

Tokens are distributed to investors. This is where FCA regulations kick in.

Tokens → Distributed to → Investors (via whitelist)

You can't just sell tokens to anyone publicly. The FCA restricts this. Investors must be verified, whitelisted, and meet certain criteria (more on this below).

Investors hold tokens in crypto wallets. The tokens represent their share of the SPV.

Step 4: Rental Income Distribution

Rent is collected from tenants. After expenses (management fees, repairs, insurance, void periods), the remaining income is distributed to token holders.

Rent → SPV → Distributed to → Token Holders (pro-rata)

Distributions can be made in GBP via bank transfer, or in stablecoins like USDC. Depends on the platform.

Step 5: Secondary Trading (Optional)

In theory, you can sell your tokens to another investor. In practice, this depends on whether a secondary market exists and has liquidity.

Token Holder A → Sells → Token Holder B

Transfers are recorded on the blockchain. The whitelist ensures only verified investors can hold tokens — this is an FCA requirement.

UK Regulatory Framework

The FCA regulates cryptoassets in the UK. This isn't the Wild West. There are rules.

FCA Oversight

Key regulations currently in play:

  • CP25/28 — Fund tokenisation (consultation closed February 2026)
  • CP25/40 — Cryptoasset activities (December 2025)
  • CP25/41 — Admissions and disclosures (December 2025)
  • CP25/42 — Prudential regime (December 2025)

Timeline:

  • September 2026 — FCA "cryptoasset gateway" opens (firms can apply for authorisation)
  • October 2027 — Full regulatory regime in force

Public Offers vs. Private Placements

This is critical. The FCA distinguishes between:

Public Offers:

  • Generally restricted under FCA rules
  • Requires a prospectus (expensive, time-consuming)
  • Enhanced disclosures required

Private Placements:

  • Allowed under specific exemptions
  • Limited to certain investor categories
  • No public solicitation or advertising

Exemptions include:

  • Existing shareholders or members (e.g., Founder's Pass holders)
  • Certified high net worth individuals
  • Sophisticated investors
  • Small offers (under €8M in 12 months, with disclosures)

Retail Investor Protections

The FCA allows retail investors to participate, but with safeguards:

  • Appropriateness assessment — You must acknowledge you understand the risks
  • 10% net investable cap — Retail investors cannot allocate more than 10% of their portfolio to high-risk investments like this
  • Clear risk disclosures — No misleading promises or guaranteed returns
  • Express consent — You must actively agree to terms, not just tick a box by accident

Pros and Cons

Potential Benefits

Lower Barriers to Entry

  • Traditional: £30k+ deposit required
  • Tokenised: Potentially £50-100 minimum (varies by platform)

Liquidity (Theoretical)

  • Traditional: Months to sell a property
  • Tokenised: Potentially faster secondary trading — but this is not guaranteed

Diversification

  • Traditional: One property means all your capital in one asset
  • Tokenised: Spread capital across multiple properties with the same money

Transparency

  • Blockchain records are immutable and auditable
  • Ownership history is traceable

Potential Risks

Regulatory Uncertainty

  • FCA regime still evolving (full rules October 2027)
  • Rules may change, affecting existing structures

Liquidity Risk

  • Secondary markets may not exist or be active
  • You may not be able to sell when you want to

Technology Risk

  • Smart contract bugs (audits help, but don't eliminate risk)
  • Wallet security is your responsibility
  • Blockchain network issues

Property Risk

  • Property values can go down
  • Rental income is not guaranteed
  • Void periods, maintenance costs, unexpected repairs

Complexity

  • SPV structure adds legal layers
  • Tax treatment may be complex
  • Requires understanding of both property and crypto

UK-Specific Considerations

Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT)

The SPV pays SDLT on property purchase, including the 3% surcharge for additional properties.

This is factored into the overall investment structure. Token holders don't pay SDLT individually.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

When the property is sold:

  • SPV pays CGT (if applicable)
  • Token holders may pay CGT on their share of gains
  • Annual exempt amount: £3,000 (2026/27 tax year)

Income Tax

Rental income distributed to token holders:

  • Taxed as property income (not dividend income)
  • Reported on Self Assessment tax return
  • Rates: 20% (basic), 40% (higher), 45% (additional)

Legal Structure

Recommended setup:

  • UK Private Limited Company (Ltd)
  • Registered at Companies House
  • Directors: At least one UK resident
  • Shareholders: Token holders (via nominee structure or direct registration)

Frequently Asked Questions

Is property tokenisation legal in the UK?

Yes, but subject to FCA regulations. Private placements are allowed under exemptions. Public offers require a prospectus or specific exemption.

Do I need to be a certified investor?

Not necessarily. Retail investors can participate with appropriate safeguards (appropriateness assessment, 10% cap, risk disclosures).

Can I lose my investment?

Yes. Property values can go down. Rental income is not guaranteed. You may lose some or all of your capital.

Is this the same as a REIT?

No. REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts) are listed companies with specific tax treatment. Tokenised property is direct ownership via an SPV structure.

Can I sell my tokens anytime?

Not necessarily. Secondary markets may not exist or be active. Liquidity is theoretical until proven.

What blockchain is used?

Varies by platform. Common choices: Ethereum, Polygon, Base. The blockchain should support transfer restrictions (whitelist) for FCA compliance.

Do I get voting rights?

Depends on the structure. Some platforms offer voting on major decisions (property sale, refinancing). Others are passive ownership only.
